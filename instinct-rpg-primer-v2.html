Instinct RPG

Everything you need before your first scene. Your GM teaches the rest at the table.

✦   ✦   ✦
The rules are lighter than they look. Read this once — not three times — then play. Most of it clicks faster at the table than on the page.
You are not expected to know everything upfront

Contests, specialized check types, and terms like Threat or Resolve — your GM will walk you through those when they happen. This primer covers what you need to sit down and play. When something new appears, ask — that’s normal.

01

What This Game Is

“The core of the game is giving people funny excuses
to use their cards in unexpected ways.”
— a conspicuously and fiendishly chaotic playtester, June 2026

Instinct RPG is a card-driven tabletop RPG. You describe what your character does. The cards confirm what that action is capable of achieving. That’s it.

No stat sheet, no ability-score math, no spell slots. You have a hand of cards that changes every scene — and a table working together to make each moment worth remembering.

02

Your Character & Their Instincts

Your Deck
Your Deck = Your Character. The full person — every card that makes up who they are.
Your Hand
Your Hand = Their Instincts Right Now. Only what surfaces in this scene — under right-now pressure.

Your deck is the full person — every card that makes up who they are. Your hand is only a slice of that person in this scene: the Instincts, bonds, and abilities surfacing under right-now pressure.

You never have your whole character available at once. That’s the point. Each scene you draw a fresh hand (typically 5 cards). You don’t choose what shows up — you discover what your character wants to do, then narrate why or how they act on it. The fun is justifying the weird hand you got, not optimizing a build.

The game is named for this. Your character’s Instincts are telling a story through you — and you’re along for the ride, freestyling how it looks at the table.

At scene end, the hand is discarded and you draw again. What drove them last scene might be gone next scene. That’s the game.

When hands run low

As a scene goes on, hands thin out. When the party’s average hand drops to about 2 cards, expect the GM to transition or close the scene. An empty hand is a pacing signal, not a punishment.

03

Before You Roll — Setting the Objective

Before any dice hit the table, the whole table agrees on what you’re actually trying to do this scene — “get out of the cell,” “grab the keys,” “calm him down enough that he backs off.” Say it out loud so everyone heard the same thing.

Then the GM tells you what success looks like before you commit to anything. Sometimes more than one approach works — talking your way through and fighting your way through might both clear the same objective, just differently. If you’re not sure which lane you’re in, ask.

The GM’s dice are the objective

Difficulty in this game isn’t a hidden number — it’s the GM’s opposing dice pool, placed on the table where you can see it. Clear it, and you did the thing you set out to do. Some GM dice left over: partial success, and the GM tells you what you got and what you didn’t.

Big objectives sometimes come in waves — separate rows of GM dice you clear one at a time, front row first. Your GM will tell you when a new wave opens.

Found a smarter way in than the one the GM expected? Say so. If it fits the fiction, the GM can open a smaller objective for exactly your plan instead of ruling it doesn’t count.

04

Basic Checks

Roll only when the outcome is genuinely uncertain. You build a pool of d6s and roll against the GM’s opposing pool on the table — the same objective pool from the last section. Then you match your dice against theirs. That’s the core of resolution.

Build

Your base pool at Tier 1 (levels 1–3) is 2d6.

Boost dice can come from card effects — and sometimes from the GM rewarding sharp play or great roleplay. Each Boost adds dice to your pool.

The GM places their opposing dice before you roll — you can see what you’re up against.

Aid

One ally discards a card, describes how it helps your check (not the card’s normal effect — GM agrees), and adds 1 die. That’s Aid.

Only one ally per check. No self-Aid.

Roll

Roll all your dice at once. Don’t rearrange until you’ve read them.

Misses — the 1s

Any die showing a natural 1 is a Miss. Remove it immediately — it won’t help clear the GM’s pool.

Crits — the 6s

Any die showing a 6 is a Crit. It explodes: roll one bonus die and add it to your pool. A bonus die showing 6 counts as another Crit but does not explode again.

Read

Toll. Each Miss (each 1 you removed) gives the GM 1 Toll — whether you succeed or not.

Flourishes. Count your Crits (dice still showing 6). Each Crit lets you gain one Flourish from the ability card you played — bonus options listed on the card. You don’t give up your 6s; you’re just counting them.

Clear the pool. Assign your remaining dice against the GM’s dice:

• If your die shows equal or higher than a GM die, remove both.
• If yours is lower, you may reduce that GM die by 1 and remove yours.

Repeat until you’re out of dice or GM dice. Clear them all: success. Some GM dice remain: partial.

Walkthrough example

A Barbarian is blocked by a heavy barred door. They play Break from hand — an Athletics check to smash part of the scene. Their Fighter ally discards a related card — say, an Instinct like Bold — and describes how they’re helping: bracing the frame, shouting encouragement, clearing debris. They’re not using that card’s normal effect; they’re spending it to Aid, and the GM agrees. That adds 1 die. That’s 3d6 on the roll: 2d6 base at Tier 1, plus 1 from Aid. 05

Spending a Flourish

A Crit isn’t just a bigger number — it’s story punctuation. Once you’ve counted your Crits, here’s the whole procedure for spending them:

Step 1

Look at the ability card you just played. Its Flourish options are printed right on the card, each behind a bold keyword.

Step 2

For each Crit you have, pick one Flourish and ask the table: “Does this fit what we’re trying to do right now?”

Step 3

If the table agrees it fits, apply the card’s printed effect exactly as written — no more, no less. The GM only judges whether it fits the fiction, never how big it is.

Flourishes come in three colors, so you can read what kind of moment you’re about to make before you even read the words:

Red — offensive. Knocks dice off the objective or enemy pool (e.g. a Flourish printed Exposed 2 removes 2 dice).
Blue — defensive. Helps an ally — Boost, redirected Threat, and the like.
Green — resolve. Heals, sheds Threat, or recovers Resolve/Hit Dice in the fiction.

You never lose the 6s that earned these — you’re counting them, not spending them away.

06

Health — Hit Dice

Hit Dice are your health — actual d6s on the table, not a number on a sheet. We never say “HP.”

When you take damage, Hit Dice are removed or reduced. When your last Hit Die is gone, you’re out of the fight. Damage and recovery details come up in play — your GM will walk you through the first time it matters.

Cards may reference Resolve or Threat during high-stakes scenes. Tap those in the glossary for a quick definition — your GM teaches how they work in the moment.

07

Your Card Types

There are a lot of card types — that’s on purpose. They let your character be expressive. Every card explains itself; read the card to learn how it works. You don’t need this list memorized.

Instinct
A single word — Bold, Perceptive, Fierce. Reveal when that quality drives an Action; choose benefit (Boost 2) or hinder (Draw 2). GM agrees either way.
Act
Your primary move. One per spotlight moment. Cost: 1.
React
Responses and interrupts — any time, in reaction to something. Cost: 0. Some carry Mill (swap at scene start).
Core
Your class’s permanent rules — usually at least 2 cards. Place them face-up in front of you; they never shuffle into the deck.
Background
Your past and what it taught you. Fires when the fiction calls on that expertise.
Ancestry
Where you come from. Fires when heritage does something useful.
Bond
A connection you name in the moment — you and the other person each gain Boost 1.
Connection
A card about you — write your own character’s name on it at session start. It shuffles into other players’ decks so they can connect with your character in play.
Item
Gear shuffled into your deck like any other card. Many carry Mill — swap at scene start if the scene doesn’t call for it.
Reading the corners of a card

Top-left badge is the card’s type (Ability, Core, Background, and so on). Top-right badge is its cost: Act spends your one action this turn, React is free and can fire any time its trigger is right, and blank means it’s a Core — always active, never played.

A gold Crit marker on a Flourish line is just a reminder that option is a Crit spend (see below) — it isn’t something you can use any other way.

08

How a Card Plays

Your Hand Shows What Your Character Wants To Do.
You Show The Table How.

Glance at your hand. Something there is pulling at your character — an Instinct, a Bond, an ability itching to fire. Describe what they do in the scene. Then play the card that matches.

If nothing in your hand fits what you described, the action can still happen as a basic check — just without card effects.

Many cards have a Trigger line: a plain-language “when you can play this.” If what’s happening in the scene matches that moment, you’re good. When in doubt, ask the GM.

Play off-card, on purpose

A card’s type is a suggestion, not a cage. A punchy Barbarian card can trigger in the middle of a tense negotiation; a quiet Bard card can trigger mid-brawl — if the fiction supports it, the GM says yes. Especially in your first scene: if a “wrong” card fits what’s happening, play it. That’s the game working as intended, not a workaround.

Your hand can also set your character’s mood this scene, not just their options. A hand full of Bonds might mean they’re feeling connected and protective today; a hand thin on anything but sharp Instincts might mean they’re on edge. Let the shape of the hand color how you play the scene — even on cards you never end up playing.

Impact Keyword Glossary

Tap any term to expand. These are the load-bearing words on your cards — filled color pills everywhere they appear.

Check ▼

Any uncertain action resolved with dice. You build a pool, roll against the GM’s opposing pool, read Misses and Crits, then clear dice. Most checks work the same way — some cards use a shorter format your GM explains when it comes up.

Boost N ▼

Add dice to a check pool — from card effects, Aid, or sometimes the GM rewarding creative play or strong roleplay. Bonus dice expire at scene end. A Boost die showing a 1 is removed like any other Miss.

Aid ▼

An ally discards a card — Instinct, Ability, or Item — and describes how it’s relevant to your check. They’re not playing the card for its normal effect; they’re narrating how it helps you, and the GM agrees. That adds 1 die to your pool. Capped at one ally per check. You cannot Aid yourself.

Miss ▼

Strictly a die showing 1. Removed immediately; gives the GM 1 Toll. A partial result — some GM dice left uncleared — is not a Miss.

Toll ▼

GM currency from Misses. Spent to escalate scenes — complications, NPC reactions, sharper consequences. The table should always feel the world responding.

Crit ▼

After a check, count dice still showing 6. Each one lets you gain one Flourish option from the ability card you played. You keep your 6s on the table — you’re counting them, not giving them up.

Flourish ▼

A bonus line listed on an ability card — not a die result. When you earn Crits, you may gain Flourishes from the card you played. One Crit typically unlocks one Flourish.

Hit Dice ▼

Physical d6s on the table — never “HP.” Damage removes or reduces them. When you’re out of Hit Dice, you’re out of the fight.

Resolve ▼

A temporary defensive layer — composure, armor, momentum — that absorbs incoming pressure before Hit Dice take damage.

Your GM sets this up and walks you through it the first time a high-stakes scene opens. You don’t need the full procedure memorized now.
Contest ▼

The scene-level container around a fight or a heated confrontation — bigger than a single Check. A Deadly Contest risks your Hit Dice; a Social Contest risks your Resolve instead. Both run on the exact same Check engine underneath: build a pool, roll, clear dice.

Your GM names the Contest type when one opens. Not required reading before session one.
Threat ▼

The GM’s offensive pool — damage or pressure the scene will deal, placed visibly before you commit to acting. It’s built and read exactly like a check, just from the GM’s side.

Taught at the table when your first Contest-style scene begins. Not required reading before session one.
Mill ▼

On Item cards and some Reacts: at scene start, you may discard this card to draw 1. Use it when the scene doesn’t call for what you’re holding.

Snap Check ▼

Some React cards call for a fast roll — 1d6, no full pool. Same Miss (1) and Crit (6) rules. Outcomes are printed on the card.

Your GM explains the first time a card asks for one. Not on your pre-session checklist.

FAQ

Questions that came up in playtests — tap to expand.

Do I need to know all the rules before we start? ▼

No. Read this primer once, then play. Your GM teaches Contests, Threat, Resolve, and anything else when it comes up. Pick up cards because they sound cool — the GM will tell you how to resolve them.

What if I don’t have the “right” card in my hand? ▼

That’s normal — you never have your whole character at once. Describe what you want to do anyway. If nothing fits, it can still happen as a basic check without card effects. The fun is making the hand you do have matter.

Can I play a card just because I want to? ▼

Yes — if what’s happening in the scene matches the card’s Trigger, you’re in good shape. Look at your hand first and narrate from there. When you’re not sure whether the moment fits, ask the GM.

Does my weapon or build matter mechanically? ▼

Not in the way most RPGs mean it. There’s no stat sheet, no gear slots to track, no map grid. Say what your character does; cards and checks handle the rest. Class Core cards cover class-specific equipment fantasies when they matter.

What’s on my Connection card — whose name goes there? ▼

Your own character’s name. That card goes into other players’ decks so they can find moments to connect with you in play.

Can I Aid myself? ▼

No. Aid must come from an ally who discards a card and describes how it helps you — not using the card’s normal effect. Only one ally can Aid a single check.

Is a partial result a failure? ▼

No — and a Miss (a natural 1) isn’t a full stop either. Partial means some GM dice remained after you assigned all yours; the GM says what you achieved and what didn’t land. The scene keeps moving.

What’s the difference between a Check, a Contest, and Threat? ▼

A Check is any single roll — build a pool, clear dice, done; it’s the entire game’s core resolution and you already know how to run one. A Contest is the bigger scene around it: a Deadly Contest risks your Hit Dice, a Social Contest risks your Resolve, but both are made of ordinary Checks. Threat is just the GM’s half of a Contest — their offensive pool, revealed before you commit, built and read exactly like a Check from your side. You don’t need to memorize this; your GM names each one as it shows up. Quick definitions live in the glossary.

What does a first combat round actually look like? ▼

Say your party stumbles into a guard patrol. The GM reveals Threat up front — the damage this fight could deal — so you can see the risk before anyone commits. You choose: fight (accepting that Threat), flee, or try to talk your way out. The GM also sets the fight’s objective pool (see the objective section above) — clearing that is how you win the scene, not just chipping away at guards one at a time. There’s no fixed turn order: once Threat is on the table, whoever’s action makes sense goes next, decided together as a table. Your GM sets up your Resolve before the first blow lands and walks you through the rest live — this is just the shape of it going in.

Some Instinct words feel abstract — how do I actually play one? ▼

Instincts are meant to be interpreted, not defined — there’s no rulebook entry for what a single word “means.” If a word doesn’t suggest an obvious action, describe your character doing something in that manner: a drier, sharper Instinct might show up as a joke at the worst possible moment, or refusing to take the danger seriously out loud. Any reading the table buys is a correct one. Stuck? Ask the table what it makes them picture — someone usually has a read on it.

When does a scene end? ▼

When the dramatic question is answered, or when hands are running low. If the party’s average hand drops to about 2 cards, expect a transition. Your GM may also telegraph when a scene is winding down.

What if a card in my hand seems useless this scene? ▼

Hold it — scenes end and hands refresh. Some Items and Reacts have Mill, letting you swap the card at scene start if it’s not what you need. Otherwise, lean into the constraint; that’s where the story gets interesting.

Do I need a character sheet? ▼

No. Your deck is your character. Core cards sit in front of you; everything else cycles through your hand.
