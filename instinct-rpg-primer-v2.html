Instinct RPG

Read this before your first session. Then put it away and play.

✦   ✦   ✦
The rules are lighter than they look. One mechanic runs everything. Most of it will click faster at the table than on the page — so read this once, not three times, and trust that the game will explain itself as it happens.
01

What This Game Is

The core of the game is giving people funny excuses
to use their cards in unexpected ways.
— Ben, Playtest #1

Instinct RPG is a card-driven tabletop RPG. You describe what your character does. The cards confirm what that action is capable of achieving. That’s it.

There’s no stat sheet to fill out, no ability score math, no tracking spell slots. What you have instead is a hand of cards that changes every scene — and a table working together to make each moment worth remembering.

02

Your Hand of Cards

Your Deck
Who your character is — their abilities, past, relationships, personality, and pressure points.
Your Hand
Who they are right now, in this scene, under this specific pressure. This is what you play with.

Those aren’t the same thing. At the start of each scene, you draw a hand of cards (typically 5). That hand is what’s live. You don’t get to choose what arrives — you choose what to do with it. At scene end, everything is discarded and you draw fresh.

An Instinct in your hand means that quality is active right now. A Bond means that connection is present and ready to be used. A Flaw means that pressure is being felt. The hand is a portrait of your character in this specific moment.

When your hand runs low

As the scene goes on, your hand will thin. When the party’s average hand drops to about 2 cards, expect the GM to transition or close the scene — an empty hand is a pacing signal, not a punishment.

An Instinct card is a single word naming a quality your character embodies. When that quality is driving an action, reveal it and gain Boost 2 — two extra dice.

03

How a Card Plays

Describe the action. Then find the card.
You never play a card cold. Description always comes first.

Say what your character is doing — what they attempt, how they move, what they reach for. Then look at your hand and ask: does any card here fit what I just described? If it does, play it. The card confirms what that action is capable of and tells you what to roll.

That order doesn’t reverse. You don’t pick up a card and work backward to justify it. If no card fits, the action can still happen — it just resolves as a basic check without card mechanics attached.

A card triggers when the fiction supports it. Read a trigger and you should immediately know whether this scene qualifies. Triggers are soft gates, not rigid conditions.

After every roll, before narration, there is a brief beat where React cards may be played. At cost 0, they require nothing — just the right moment.

A React fires in response — here, when you’ve just been hit. Cost 0 means it doesn’t spend your turn. The trigger tells you exactly when it’s valid.

04

Making a Check

Roll only when the outcome is genuinely uncertain. Every check — combat or non-combat — follows one model: you roll a pool of d6s against the GM’s opposing pool. Your dice clear theirs one at a time. That’s the entire game’s resolution.

Build

Your base pool at Tier 1 (levels 1–3) is 2d6.

Cards can add dice as Boost N — “N” more dice in the pool, from card effects. One ally may also discard a card to add 1 die to your roll; this is called Aid. Only one ally can Aid a single check, and you cannot Aid your own roll.

The GM places their opposing dice pool visibly on the table before you roll. Difficulty is made physical — you can see exactly what you’re up against.

Roll

Roll all your dice simultaneously. Don’t touch the results until you’ve read them.

Read

Step 1 — Pull the 1s. Any die showing a natural 1 is a Miss. Remove it immediately. Each Miss gives the GM 1 Toll right now — whether the check succeeds or fails overall.

Step 2 — Count the 6s. Any die showing a 6 is a Flourish. It explodes: roll one bonus die and add it. A bonus die showing 6 earns another Flourish but doesn’t explode again. Count Flourishes earned — that’s how many bonus options you may spend from your card.

Step 3 — Clear the opposing pool. Your remaining dice clear the GM’s dice one at a time. Clear them all: full success. Some remain: GM adjudicates what was and wasn’t achieved.

Pool Cleared
Full success. Narrate what you pull off. Spend any Flourishes on your card’s bonus options.
Partial
Some GM dice remain. The GM adjudicates what was and wasn’t achieved. This is not a Miss — it’s a partial result.
Miss (the 1s)
Already removed in Step 1. The GM gains Toll. The scene keeps moving — a Miss is never a stop.

05

Reading Your Cards

Cards use three levels of visual weight to tell you what kind of word you’re looking at.

Impact keywords carry real mechanical effects — filled color pills everywhere they appear:

Boost N
Add N dice to a pool. Expires at scene end; a Boost die showing 1 is removed.
Flourish
A 6 on any die. Unlocks spend options on your card. Count them after all dice resolve.
Miss
A natural 1 only. Gives the GM 1 Toll. A partial result is not a Miss.
Toll
GM resource generated by Misses. Spent to make the world react — complications, NPC moves, escalations.
Resolve
Your temporary defensive buffer — absorbs damage before your Hit Dice. Set up at Contest start.
Threat
The GM’s offensive check — damage an encounter will deal, revealed upfront before you commit.
Hit Dice
Your health pool — physical d6s on the table. Never “HP.”
Aid
An ally discards a card to add 1 die to your check. One ally per check. No self-Aid.
Mill
At scene start, you may discard this card to draw 1. Appears on Item and some React cards.

Index keywords classify a card for sorting — card types (Instinct Act React) and class names. No gameplay effect.

Narrative vocabulary — Exposed, Rattled, Rooted — gives the table a shared word for something in the fiction. These carry no enforced mechanics. What they mean is the GM’s call. Written in plain bold, never as pills.

06

Your Card Types

Instinct
A single word — Bold, Perceptive, Fierce — naming a quality driving you right now. Reveal when that quality is at work. Gain Boost 2.
Act
Your primary move. Play one on your turn. Cost: 1 (your spotlight for this moment).
React
Interrupts and responses. Play any time, in response to any action. Cost: 0. Cards with Mill can be swapped at scene start.
Core
Your class’s permanent rules. Sits face-up in your Active area and never enters the deck. Always available, every scene.
Background
Your character’s past and what it taught them. Fires when the fiction calls on that expertise.
Ancestry
Where you come from. Fires when your heritage does something useful at the right moment.
Bond
Your connection to someone. You name the moment — describe how this relationship is driving the action — and you both gain Boost 1.
Connection
A Bond to a specific named party member, filled in at session start. Includes special reward options and an Aid clause. Shuffled into other players’ decks.
Flaw 🔒
Pressure you’re carrying. You hold 2 Flaws from the same pool. Find something in the scene that fits. Lean in dramatically — or suppress it at an ongoing cost. You can’t ignore a Flaw in hand.
Item
A tool, weapon, or gear. Lives in your Active area. Carries Mill — swap at scene start if it’s not what you need.

Read this when your first contest begins
Contests, Threat & Resolve
▼
You don’t need this section until the GM opens a Contest. When they do, read it — then you’ll know exactly what’s happening.

What a Contest Is

A Contest is any structured confrontation — when the stakes are high enough that the scene has two sides pushing against each other with real consequences.

Two types:

Deadly Contest
Hit Dice are at risk. This is combat. Characters can die. Same resolution engine, with the additions below.
Social Contest
Only Resolve is at risk. A tense debate, interrogation, or high-stakes confrontation. Same engine. When one side’s Resolve is depleted, the confrontation ends.

Both use the same opposing pool, Miss/Flourish/Toll system, and Open Floor structure. The only difference is what’s at stake.

Threat & Open Floor

At Contest start, the GM reveals Threat — the damage the encounter will deal — before anyone acts. Threat dice are placed visibly in front of relevant players. This is locked: Threat resolves regardless of the order players act.

This means you know what’s coming before you commit. You choose: fight (accepting the Threat), flee, or negotiate. Engaging means accepting the Threat.

Open Floor means there is no fixed turn order. After Threat is revealed, any player may act in whatever sequence makes narrative sense. The group decides moment to moment.

Defensive spillover

When your defensive dice fully clear an incoming Threat pool, any leftover dice can go to any target you choose — an ally, or yourself. Spillover isn’t restricted to others.

Still Standing fires in the window after a Strike resolves. A React at cost 0 — you keep your action.

Resolve & Hit Dice

Hit Dice are your health pool — actual d6s on the table. When your last Hit Die falls, you’re out of the fight.

At Contest start, before cards are played, each player sets up a Resolve pool — a temporary defensive layer absorbing all incoming damage before it touches Hit Dice. Establish Resolve by rolling a check (you can Miss or Flourish on it). When Resolve is depleted, damage goes to Hit Dice directly.

Resolve works in Social Contests too — composure, credibility, emotional momentum. The engine is identical.

Hit Dice are fully removed when spent

When a Hit Die is spent to absorb damage, it’s removed completely — no partial shave. Enemy Hit Dice are placed visibly on the table at Contest start so you can see what you’re fighting.

Reframe is an Act card — one action, your spotlight. It can shift a Deadly Contest into a Social Contest mid-scene. Cards have no scene-type restrictions.

07

Building Your Deck

Character creation takes about an hour and is itself play. You’re not filling out a sheet — you’re building a story through the cards you choose and the stems you complete.

Your completed deck will have roughly 20 cards. Here’s the order:

1
Choose a Background (1 of 12)
What did your character do before the story started? Each Background carries a past-expertise trigger and a choose-one effect. Complete the ORIGIN stem on the card — a first-person, past-tense sentence only your character can finish.
2
Choose an Ancestry (1 of 10)
Human, Elf, Dwarf, Half-Orc, Tiefling, and more. Each Ancestry has a heritage trigger and a choose-one effect. Complete the ORIGIN stem.
3
Bond & Connection Cards
Bonds go in your deck — personal connections that fire when the moment finds them. You and the named person each gain Boost 1.
Connection cards are filled in at session start with a party member’s name, then shuffled into other players’ decks. If someone plays your Connection, both of you choose a reward.
4
Draw 2 Flaws
Both Flaws come from the same pool. Each has a stem you complete at the table: “___ sets me off.” or “I won’t back down when ___.” When a Flaw fires: lean in dramatically (GM makes a free move) or suppress it (pay an ongoing cost while you hold it).
5
Draw 5 Instincts at random
You don’t choose your Instincts — you draw them. Bold, Cunning, Fierce, Steadfast, Empathic… whatever arrives is who your character is right now. Complete the ORIGIN stem on each one.
6 Instinct cards will be in your final 20-card deck. Expect roughly one Instinct per scene.
6
Choose 1 Item
A weapon, tool, or gear your character carries. Item cards go in your Active area (not the deck). Most carry Mill — swap at scene start if the scene doesn’t call for it.
7
Add your Class deck
Your Acts, Reacts, and Core card. The Core goes face-up in your Active area. The Acts and Reacts make up the bulk of your 20-card deck.
