Instinct RPG

For first-time players at the table tonight

✦   ✦   ✦
Here's what you need to know before your first session. The rules are lighter than they look, and most of the mechanics will click faster at the table than they do on the page. Read through once — then dive in.
01

What Kind of Game Is This?

Instinct RPG is a card-driven tabletop RPG. The cards in your hand at any moment represent who your character is right now, and your job is to wield that hand as faithfully and as creatively as your character would. Three things drive every good session:

Three pillars of play

Craftiness. There's real art in reading your hand, spotting the opening, and making a play that threads your character's situation through the scene in a way nobody anticipated. Not because it's clever — because it's right for this moment.

Character in the moment. Whatever's in your hand is alive and present right now. The game is about harnessing those qualities as they surface, not executing a plan you mapped out before you sat down.

Story always moving forward. Every roll, every card, every failure is fuel. The scene never stops. Your job isn't to survive the moment — it's to make it worth remembering.

The Barbarian who throws herself between an ally and a verbal attack, because someone is getting torn apart and she's not going to let that happen. The Rogue who holds back when everyone else charges — patience is the weapon, and waiting for the right moment pays off differently than rushing in. The Warlock who leans on a debt they're owed, not because it's the safe play, but because whatever they bargained with is watching and the terms are the terms. These are the moments the game is built for.

02

Your Deck, Your Hand

Your Deck
Who your character is across all sessions — their abilities, their history, their relationships and pressures.
Your Hand
Who they are right now, in this scene, under this specific pressure. This is what you play with.

Those aren't the same thing. A character in a story doesn't have equal access to every part of themselves at every moment. Some scenes you're sharp and ready. Some scenes an old wound surfaces. Some scenes a quality you've been holding back shows up in your hand and demands to be used. The hand is what's live — that's what you work with. You don't get to choose what arrives in each scene. You get to choose what to do with it.

03

How Scenes Work

A scene is the basic unit of play. Each one has a location, a set of active participants, and a dramatic question — something the table is working through or pushing past. When the dramatic question is answered, avoided, or transformed into something new, the scene ends and a new one begins.

At scene start, you draw up to 5 cards. That hand is what you have for the entire scene. There's no drawing mid-scene unless a card explicitly lets you. What you drew is what you have to work with.

At scene end, your hand is discarded. Everything you held — played or not — goes to your discard pile. You draw a fresh hand of 5 for the next scene. Conditions applied during the scene typically clear, unless the GM decides they carry forward.

The game recognizes five scene types:

Combat Social Exploration Rest & Downtime Party Scenes

Cards don't have scene restrictions. A card that reads like a combat move can fire in a negotiation if the fiction calls for it. Scene type is context, not a filter on your options.

04

How Checks Work

A check is the game's core resolution mechanic. Nearly every uncertain action — striking an enemy, persuading a guard, navigating a hazard, holding your composure — is resolved as a check. When a card says "perform a check" or the GM calls for one, the same rules always apply.

Every check follows three phases: Gather → Roll → Read Your Dice.

Gather

At Tier 1 (Levels 1–3), your base pool is 2d6.

Before rolling, any player at the table — including yourself — may discard one card from their hand to add one d6 to the active player's pool. Discarding to add to someone else's roll is called Aid. Multiple players can Aid the same roll.

Declare your full pool before picking up the dice. No changes once results are visible.

Roll

Roll all dice simultaneously.

Read Your Dice

Three steps, in order:

1
Miss Any die showing 1 is a Miss die. Remove it from the pool. Each Miss die gives the GM 1 Toll — immediately, regardless of whether you succeed or fail overall.
2
Crit Any die showing 6 is a Crit die. Each explodes — roll one bonus die and add it to the pool. On success, Crit dice also let you spend Flourishes on your card's bonus options.
3
Total Add everything remaining. Compare to the GM's DC. That's your result.
Beat the DC
Success. Narrate what you pull off. Spend any Flourishes earned from Crit dice on your card's bonus options.
Below the DC
Failure — but the scene always keeps moving. The GM takes it somewhere interesting.
05

What Toll Is

Every Miss die — any die showing 1 — gives the GM 1 Toll, whether the overall check succeeds or fails.

Toll is the GM's scene-pressure resource. It isn't spent to punish you specifically — it's spent to make the world react. A guard appears. A negotiation shifts. An NPC makes a move they'd been holding back. Toll is what keeps even successful moments feeling earned.

Failure is not a stop

When you fail a check, the GM gains Toll and the fiction advances anyway — toward something more interesting than nothing. Failure is the GM's turn, not your punishment. The scene always goes somewhere.

06

Resolve & Hit Dice

Hit Dice are your health pool — physical dice on the table in front of you, mostly d6s. Enemies deal damage equal to their dice face values. When you take damage, your Hit Dice are removed or reduced. At Level 1, all your Hit Dice start at their maximum value.

Resolve is a temporary defensive pool set up at the start of any high-stakes encounter — a fight, an intense confrontation, a scene where the consequences are real and immediate. When the GM calls a high-stakes encounter, each player makes a check and places those resulting dice in front of them as their Resolve pool. Resolve absorbs all incoming damage before anything touches your Hit Dice. When it runs dry, consequences start hitting your actual health.

Resolve works in social encounters too

A tense interrogation, a high-stakes debate, a scene where someone is actively working to break your character down — these can all be high-stakes encounters with a Resolve pool. Composure, credibility, momentum under pressure — they work exactly the same way as taking a hit. The game doesn't draw a hard line between physical and social stakes. The same mechanics handle both.

You'll practice the full rhythm of combat and Resolve at the table — the rules will make more sense in motion than on paper. One thing to know now: the game always shows you the stakes. Enemy dice are placed visibly on the table before you commit to a fight. You see what's coming and choose whether to engage.

Group skill checks work on the same logic. When the whole party faces a shared obstacle — a timed objective, a complex mechanism, a scene that needs every hand — the GM builds a difficulty pool (2d6 per player) and places those dice on the table. Each player takes one action to reduce it. The goal is to clear the pool in a single round. The mechanic is the same: you're looking at physical dice representing a challenge, and working together to remove them.

07

High-Stakes Encounters

Some scenes carry real, immediate stakes — a fight, an intense confrontation, a performance where failure lands hard. These are high-stakes encounters, and they run on a distinct structure.

Setup. Every involved player makes a Resolve check and places those dice in front of themselves as their buffer. The GM places the Objectives — the dice representing what the party must clear to end the encounter.

Each round runs three steps:

Threat

Each player rolls their Threat dice face-up. The amount scales with the encounter. Narrate what's pressing in on you — the enemy singling you out, terrain working against you, the boss locking eyes with you.

Action

Each player takes one action, simultaneously in any order. Choose how to spend it:

Offensive
Roll against the Objectives to clear them and progress the encounter.
Defensive
Roll against your own Threat dice to reduce incoming damage — or redirect that protection to an ally.

The GM may allow a single action to count toward both.

Consequence

Any Threat dice left untouched hit your Resolve. When all waves of Objectives are cleared, the encounter ends.

When Resolve breaks

Any remaining Threat from that round hits your Hit Dice. Then you choose: retreat — you're safe, but out of the fight for the rest of the encounter — or stay in — you keep acting, but all future Threat hits your Hit Dice directly.

Knockout. If your last Hit Die falls, you're out. You take a Scar — a permanent card added to your deck. Three Scars and your character dies or retires (the GM and story decide how).

08

Your Cards

Eight card types live in your deck. Here's the quick guide:

Core
Your class's permanent rules. These sit face-up in your Active area and never enter the deck. Always available, every scene.
Act
Your primary moves. Play one on your turn. Cost: 1 — playing this card is your spotlight for this moment.
React
Interrupts and responses. Cost: 0. Play any time, in response to any action at the table. There's a brief React window after every roll.
Instinct
A single word — Bold, Cautious, Perceptive, Loyal — naming a quality that's driving you right now. Reveal from hand when that quality is at work. Gain Boost 2 — two extra dice.
Background
Your character's past and what it taught them. Plays when the fictional trigger fires — a moment of expertise, lucky knowledge, or a sharp read on the situation.
Ancestry
Where you come from. Plays when the trigger fires — your heritage does something useful at the right moment.
Bond
Your connection to someone. Find the moment — name it aloud — and both you and the named person gain Boost 1.
Flaw 🔒
The pressure you're under right now. Find something in the scene that fits. Lean in dramatically (GM makes a free move) or suppress it (pay an ongoing cost while you hold the card). You can't simply ignore a Flaw in hand.

The Setup

Fiction First

10

The Golden Rule

Describe the action. Then find the card.
You never play a card cold. The description always comes first.

You say what your character is doing — what they attempt, what they reach for, how they move. Then you look at your hand and ask: does any card here fit what I just described? If it does, play it. The card confirms what that action is capable of achieving and tells you what to roll.

That order doesn't reverse. You don't pick up a card and work backward to justify it. You describe first, find the card second. If no card fits, the action can still happen — it just resolves as a basic check without card mechanics attached.

Your cards aren't a menu. They're a hand. Look at what you're holding and ask what your character would actually do right now — then find the card that earns the mechanics for it.
