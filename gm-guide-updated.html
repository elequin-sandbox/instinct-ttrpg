01

Your Role

You are the world. You describe what the characters encounter, adjudicate what their actions achieve, and keep the fiction moving forward. You do not play against the players — you play the world, which has its own logic and pressures and will.

Fiction first. Before any roll, the fiction has to call for it. If a player narrates something and the outcome is obvious given the scene, it just happens — no roll needed. Only reach for dice when the outcome is genuinely uncertain and the stakes of that uncertainty matter. Every roll generates Toll. Make sure the roll is worth it.

A Miss is never a stop. When a check produces Miss dice (natural 1s), you gain Toll and the scene advances — somewhere more interesting than nothing. Miss is your turn, not the player's punishment. Give them something to react to.

The north star. Ben, the first playtester, said it best: "The core of the game is giving people funny excuses to use their cards in unexpected ways." Every ruling, every scene, every NPC should be measured against that. If something makes it harder to use cards unexpectedly, reconsider it.

Your three primary moves

Make the world react. Spend Toll to escalate, introduce complications, or give NPCs moves they've been holding back. Every player action should change something in the world — including successful ones.

Make the stakes visible. Place opposing dice on the table before players commit. Show them what they're walking into. Deadly Contests are tactical decisions with known costs, not ambushes.

Read the room by reading the hands. Watch card counts. When hands run thin, the scene is ready to turn or close. Player card economy is your pacing meter.

02

Building Opposition — The Dice Pool

There is no DC ladder or hidden target number in Instinct RPG. Difficulty is represented by a physical opposing dice pool placed on the table before the roll. Player dice clear opposing dice one at a time. When all opposing dice are cleared, the check succeeds. Partial resolution (some dice remain) = partial success; the GM adjudicates what was and wasn't achieved.

This applies to every check — exploration, social, combat, investigation, skill challenges. One engine, always visible.

Random Method

Roll dice appropriate to the scene's danger level and let them land. The resulting face values become the opposition. Fast and generates unpredictability — even you don't know exactly how hard this moment will be.

Hand-Placed Method

Select specific die values before placing them. Use this when precise difficulty matters — a specific obstacle with a known challenge level, or a boss who needs to hit differently than the mooks around them. A named villain can be hand-placed as a d10 showing 9, while generic enemies around them are randomly rolled d6s. Same table, same Contest, legibly differentiated. No separate stat blocks needed.

You can combine both methods in a single pool: hand-place a die for the dominant threat and randomly roll the rest for the chaos around it.

Difficulty Feel Suggested Pool
Easy 1–2 dice, low values (2–3 on a d6)
Moderate 2–3 mixed dice
Hard 3–4 dice, some high values
Very Hard 4–5 dice, mostly high values
Boss / Climactic Mixed pool with hand-placed high-face dice for named threats alongside random dice for everything else
GM Dice — Use a Visually Distinct Color

Your dice should be immediately visually different from the players' dice. Red dice are the recommended choice. When your opposing pool is sitting on the table next to player dice, it should be unmistakably clear which are which. This isn't just aesthetics — it eliminates mistakes at a busy table and reinforces that the opposing pool is a distinct object the players are trying to clear.

03

Toll

[Toll] is generated by Miss dice — any die in a player's pool that rolls a natural 1. Each 1 = 1 Toll added to your pool, immediately, regardless of whether the check otherwise succeeds. A player can roll brilliantly and still give you Toll if their pool contained a 1.

Toll is your scene-pressure resource. It is not a punishment mechanism — it is the world reacting. Spend it to make things more interesting, not to make things more miserable.

Escalate
A new element enters the scene. Reinforcements arrive. A door opens. Something the party thought settled turns out not to be.
NPC Reaction
An NPC makes a move they'd been holding back. An ally's hesitation becomes obvious. An enemy smells opportunity.
Complication
The environment shifts. A resource becomes contested. Time pressure materializes. The thing they succeeded at comes with an asterisk.
Enemy Ability
A named NPC activates a special move or trump card. Reserve this for significant moments — Toll makes it feel earned.

Toll accumulates across the scene. You don't have to spend it immediately. Sitting on unspent Toll is a legitimate pressure the players can feel — they know you're holding something. Use it when the moment is right.

Players can spend Toll too

Some cards reference Toll explicitly and let players spend it. If a player's card effect removes Toll from your pool, that's the system working correctly — they spent a resource to defuse pressure. Don't resist it.

04

Deadly Contests

A Deadly Contest is any scene where Hit Dice can be lost and characters are eligible to die. It uses the standard resolution engine (opposing dice pool, Misses generate Toll, Crits give Flourishes) with this additional structure.

Starting a Deadly Contest

When a Deadly Contest begins, two things happen simultaneously — before anyone acts:

1
Reveal Threat. Place Threat dice visibly in front of each relevant player. This is the damage they will take from enemies — locked in at Contest start, resolved regardless of player order. Threat is not hidden. Players see exactly what's coming.
2
Resolve sets up. Each player moves a portion of their Hit Dice forward as their Resolve pool. Resolve absorbs incoming Threat before it touches actual health.

After this setup, players decide: fight (accepting the Threat they can see), flee, or negotiate. Engaging means accepting the Threat. This is a deliberate choice with known stakes.

Open Floor

Deadly Contests have no fixed turn order. After Threat is revealed, any player may act in whatever sequence makes narrative sense. The group decides who goes when, moment to moment. This is the Open Floor.

The Open Floor means Threat is resolved separately from player action order. Players don't "dodge" Threat by going first — it lands at the end of the round regardless. They can redirect it, reduce it, or absorb it through cards and defensive actions, but they can't simply outpace it.

Defensive Spillover

When a player deploys defensive dice and those dice fully clear an incoming Threat pool, any leftover dice may be directed to any target of the player's choice — including themselves. Spillover is not restricted to allies. This rewards efficient defense and creates meaningful decisions about where to send surplus protection.

NPC Threats — Two Valid Techniques

When an NPC is in harm's way, you have two legitimate approaches. Choose based on how mechanically significant that NPC's survival is to the scene.

(a) Real Threat Dice

Roll actual Threat dice against the NPC, exactly as a player would face them. This means defensive actions by players have something physical to intercept — they can spend dice to protect the NPC just like they'd protect each other. Use this when the NPC's survival matters mechanically to the scene outcome.

(b) Abstracted Severity

Give the NPC a narrative health or severity description instead of tracked dice. "Defend this NPC" actions change the narrated outcome rather than removing physical dice. Use this for NPCs whose survival is important to the story but mechanically simple to resolve — bystanders, minor allies, quest-givers in the crossfire.

Enemy Hit Dice

Place enemy Hit Dice visibly in front of the relevant player at Contest start — this is what players are working to clear. Use fixed or maximized arrangements. A guard with one d8 and two d4s is immediately legible; players can plan around it.

Prefer d6s wherever possible. Most enemies can be represented entirely in d6s. Named villains warrant d8s or d10s. The die size signals importance as much as the value.

When Resolve Breaks

Any remaining Threat that wasn't cleared hits the player's Hit Dice directly. At that point, the player chooses: retreat (they're safe but out of the fight for this Contest) or stay in (future Threat hits Hit Dice directly, no buffer). If a player's last Hit Die falls, they take a Scar — a permanent card added to their deck. Three Scars = character death or retirement (story decides).

05

Social Contests

A Social Contest is any scene where Resolve is at risk but characters are not eligible to lose Hit Dice. Tense interrogations, high-stakes debates, a verbal confrontation where someone is actively trying to break a character down — these are Social Contests.

The mechanics are identical to a Deadly Contest. Same opposing dice pool. Same Open Floor. Same Miss/Crit/Toll rules. Same Resolve setup. The only difference is the narrative register:

Threat
Pressure applied to Resolve — verbal challenges, emotional appeals, credibility attacks, rhetorical maneuvers.
Resolve
Composure, credibility, emotional momentum. Narrated per character: a Bard's wit being worn down, a Paladin's certainty being tested, a Rogue's careful fabrication unraveling.
Depletion
When one side's Resolve is gone, the confrontation ends — they concede, break, or lose the argument. Hit Dice are never at risk in a Social Contest.

Don't treat Social Contests as a softer version of Deadly Contests. The stakes are real — relationships, credibility, leverage, and narrative momentum hang on them. Give Social Contests the same structural gravity.

Cards work the same way

There are no "social cards" and "combat cards." A card that applies Rattled can do so with a cutting remark as easily as a fist. Exposed can mean socially exposed — caught in a lie, caught without a response. The table's job is to narrate why a card fits. Your job is to let them.

06

Reading the Table

Card counts are your primary pacing instrument. Watch hands, not clocks.

Full hands
Scene is fresh. Players have options. Lean into complexity — add wrinkles, introduce competing objectives, let the moment breathe.
2–3 cards avg
Scene is mid-burn. Players are starting to feel constraint. Good time for a climactic move or a turn that forces a decision.
≤ 2 cards avg
Pacing signal. Aim to transition or end the scene. Players falling below this threshold are running on fumes — mechanically and narratively. The scene has had its moment; let it close.
Empty hand
That player falls back to narrative/basic actions only — no card effects. This is survivable but should be brief. If the scene is still going, prioritize a close.

You don't have to count cards obsessively — a quick glance at how many cards each player is holding is enough. Players with thick hands should be getting screen time. Players nearly empty have already spent everything they had; they've given the scene what they could.

As hands deplete, spotlight shifts

Players with more cards will naturally dominate the later part of a scene — they have more options. This is intended. As cards deplete, the scene naturally migrates toward whoever still has resources. Honor this drift. Don't artificially force depleted players to do more than they have resources for.

07

Character Creation at the Table

Character creation takes roughly one hour and is itself a form of play. Cards have fill-in stems — prompted first-person sentences players complete at the table. Characters emerge from this process. You're not handing them a stat sheet; you're prompting them into a backstory.

Follow this order exactly:

1
Background — Player chooses 1 from the available set (12 options). What did they do before this? Completes a fill-in stem.
2
Ancestry — Player chooses 1 from the available set (10 options). Where do they come from? Completes a fill-in stem.
3
Bond / Connection — Assigned or chosen based on party setup (~2 per character). Bond stems are player-initiated — the player names the moment, names the connection. A Connection is a Bond to a specific named party member; these are earned through Rest, Growth rewards, or by discarding a Bond as a Super Aid during play.
4
Flaw — Draw 2 Flaw cards from the single shared Flaw pool. No sub-categories — it's one pool. Complete the fill-in stem for each at the table. Both Flaws go into the deck.
5
Instinct — Draw 5 Instinct cards at random from the tableau; player keeps and shuffles in 6 (accounting for one added via a different source). Each is a single word naming a quality. No stem to fill in.
6
Item — Player selects an Item card appropriate to the character. Item cards may carry the Mill keyword: at scene start, the player may discard it to draw 1. This lets them trade a situational Item for a fresh draw when the scene doesn't call for it.
7
Class Abilities — Class ability cards fill out the remaining deck slots. Target total is 20 cards. Core cards sit face-up in the Active area and never enter the deck.
Stems are soft doorways

Every fill-in stem is a prompt, not a corridor. Players should feel invited to complete them in unexpected ways. A Flaw that reads "I can't resist ___" could be anything from a vulnerability to an obsession to a compulsion nobody at the table anticipated. Let it be theirs.

Deck target: 20 cards total. Background (1) + Ancestry (1) + Bonds (~2) + Flaws (2) + Instincts (6) + Item (1) + Class ability cards (remaining to reach 20) = complete deck. Core cards don't count toward this total — they live in the Active area.

08

Class Notes for the GM

These are the mechanics most likely to create GM questions at the table. For full class detail, see the at-class-quick-ref skill.

Equipment — No Universal Keyword

There is no universal Equip keyword or equipment slot system. The "I always have my gear" fantasy is built directly into specific class Core cards, applied on a class-by-class basis. Fighter and Paladin are the current examples — their Core cards handle equipment access. If a class doesn't have a Core card that establishes equipment access, they don't have a formal equipment mechanic. Don't improvise one at the table; this is intentional.

Warlock — Patron Invocation

The Patron invocation cost works as follows. This should be clear on the card itself, but reinforce it at the table if needed:

First invocation
Free. The Warlock chooses which Mark to assign. No cost, no roll.
Later invocations
The GM rolls randomly to determine the Mark cost. The Warlock doesn't get to choose — the Patron does.

This structure makes the Patron feel genuinely unpredictable on repeated invocations without making first invocations punishing. The Warlock can plan around the first call; the Patron reasserts control on anything after.

Barbarian — Rage Pool

The Barbarian accumulates d6s in their Rage pool from two sources: Miss dice (natural 1s rolled by the Barbarian) and incoming Threat dice. These are physical dice placed on the table.

Frenzy trigger
When the Rage pool reaches 5 or more dice, the Barbarian may trigger Frenzy: grant flat Boost 3 to the frenzied action, then fully empty the Rage pool. The pool does not halve — it fully depletes.

The threshold is 5 (not 6) because Miss dice are rarer under the current Miss = natural-1-only definition. The full-depletion rule prevents chain re-triggers in the same scene.

The Mill Keyword

Mill appears on Item cards and React-subtype class ability cards only. Its text reads: "At scene start, you may discard this to draw 1."

This is the formal replacement for the previously undocumented soft Mulligan. Players can trade a situational Mill card for a fresh draw before the scene gets going. It does not apply to other card types — if a non-Mill card is in hand at scene start, it stays.

Conditions — Narrative vs. Mechanical

Most conditions (Exposed, Rattled, Rooted) are purely narrative vocabulary — no enforced mechanical fallback. What they mean in any given moment is what you decide in real time. A card may include a parenthetical (here: [interpretation]) as a suggestion — it is not a rule.

Conditions with enforced mechanical behavior: Hidden, Bolstered, Marked, Sundered. These carry specific rules — see the condition reference card. Note that Rattled is currently undefined mechanically and should not be used on new cards; what it means at the table is your call, case by case.
