## WHAT'S WORKING — DO NOT BREAK THESE

Before touching anything, internalize what the players loved. Changes should enhance, not dismantle these.

- ✅ **Aid feels great.** Stacking dice for a single player action → rolling a big pile together → everyone leans in. This is the core dopamine loop. It's working.
- ✅ **Telegraphed threat is a hit.** Players loved seeing incoming damage in advance and making a real choice: attack or defend. This is better than what you had in Playtest 1. Keep it.
- ✅ **Clearing objective dice is satisfying.** "Taking out this die" has real strategy and felt good to watch even as a bystander (confirmed by unconscious-Trillion player).
- ✅ **Empty hand = satisfied, not punished.** Every player felt good about spending their last card. The pacing mechanic (new scene when 2 cards remain) is sound in principle.
- ✅ **Story moments are emerging organically.** Rage + emotional damage from the doll, Bubbles juggling ghost swords, Cork going Yeti — these came from the cards, not from you scripting them. The system is doing its job.
- ✅ **One-shot format is this game's strongest sell.** Multiple players independently called it out. Consider leading with this in your pitch/marketing framing.

## PRIMARY CHANGES — DO THESE THIS WEEK

These are high-confidence, directly raised by multiple players, and have clear solutions you already landed on in the session debrief.

---

### [ ] P1 — Cap Aid at One Ally (No Self-Aid)

**The problem:** Aid actions are currently too cheap and stackable. At level 1, players were rolling 6–9 dice routinely. That's not level 1 — that's late-game. Scaling has nowhere to go, and players noticed.

**The fix:**
- Only **one ally** may Aid an action. The acting player **cannot Aid themselves**.
- GM inspiration still works as a separate bonus (keep it).
- This caps a standard roll at **3 dice** (2 base + 1 aid) before crits.

**Downstream effect you must do alongside this:**
- Re-evaluate DC benchmarks. With this cap, a "hard" check at level 1 should feel like a 13 again, not a 20–25 range. Communicate target DCs to players more consistently.

**What to preserve:** Discarding a card to Aid should still require that the card be contextually relevant. That friction is part of what made the Aid moments feel earned and thematic.

---

### [ ] P2 — Remove Status Effects (Rattled / Exposed / Rooted) for Now

**The problem:** Status conditions are not pulling their weight. Rattled cleared on its own before it mattered. Exposed added a die that vanished in the noise of a 9-dice pool. Rooted felt arbitrary in an abstract pooled-enemy system. Players noticed they didn't feel meaningful.

**The fix:**
- **Remove rattled, exposed, and rooted from the next playtest entirely.**
- Don't even leave them as keywords. Strip them from any card that references them for now.

**Future path:** When you re-introduce them, they should have clear, automatic effects — e.g., rattled = remove lowest threat die, exposed = attacker gets boost 1, rooted = remove player from threat targeting. Run them as a clean A/B test in a dedicated future session.

---

### [ ] P3 — Rewrite Bond Cards to Be Player-Initiated

**The problem:** Current bond cards have prerequisites the player doesn't control (e.g., "find someone who is closed off / quiet / struggling"). You can't force a moment; you can only wait for one. That makes it a dead card too often.

**The fix:** Rewrite the trigger so the *player* initiates the scene, not the circumstances.

**Draft template:**
> "Start a scene in which you [express this bond trait] toward another character. If they engage with it, you both gain Boost 1."

You're removing the waiting and putting the player in the driver's seat. They control when it fires; the other player just has to respond.

**Secondary upgrade to consider:** Bond cards may also function better as persistent core-area cards (like Patron) that go into the draw pile after use, rather than always in the deck hand. This came up and felt right — sit on it.

---

### [ ] P4 — Introduce Connection Cards (Party Name Cards)

**The idea emerged in the debrief:** At session start, every player writes their character's name on a blank bond card. These get shuffled into everyone else's decks. When you draw a card with another PC's name on it, you initiate a flashback — narrate a shared memory that's relevant to the current scene, and you both gain Boost 1 (or Boost 2 — test both).

**Why this is good:**
- Forces inter-character moments organically, not prescriptively
- The memory changes every scene because the context changes
- Works in combat ("Cork, this reminds me of when you threw that cart at a door...")
- Scales naturally with party size

**Draft card text:**
> "Connection — [Character Name]
> During this scene, narrate a memory you share with [Name] that connects to what's happening now. If you tell it to them at the table, you both gain Boost 1."

**Deck math note:** With Connection cards added + the two-flaw change (P5), you're going from ~20 to ~23–24 cards. You can trim Instincts from 5 to 4 to compensate if you want to stay at 20. That number was arbitrary anyway.

---

### [ ] P5 — Add a Second Flaw

**The case:** One flaw is too rare. You can go entire scenes without seeing it. Flaws were fun — they made memorable moments (Cork sandbaggin the show, Trillion's bog complaints getting him punched). More is better here.

**The fix:** Add a second flaw slot to the loadout. Two flaws in hand at once creates a genuine resource problem and makes the cleansing mechanic matter more.

**Optional upgrade (raised in debrief):** Consider having two distinct flaw flavors — one is a classic flaw/weakness ("I'm reckless"), one is a virtue/ideal that becomes a flaw through obsession ("I protect everyone even when I shouldn't"). This adds narrative texture without mechanical complexity. Not required for next playtest.

---

### [ ] P6 — Fix Barbarian Rage: Failures = Ones Only; Cap Frenzy at 4 Dice

**The problem:** You ruled mid-session that any die that doesn't clear an enemy die = a failure for rage purposes. This caused cascading rage (frenzy → reaction → frenzy again). Funny in a one-shot, broken as a repeatable mechanic.

**The fix:**
- **Failures for rage accumulation = ones only.** Same rule as every other part of the game.
- **Frenzy dice cap = 4.** When the rage pool hits 6, you go off; you use a max of 4 dice as Boost. This keeps frenzy powerful but not game-warping.
- Cork was a joy to watch — this just needs a single line of text to prevent the infinite loop.

---

### [ ] P7 — Rewrite Reaction Trigger Language to Match the Threat System

**The problem:** Several reaction cards (Retribution, Protect the Weak) didn't fire because their trigger conditions — "when an ally takes damage" / "when an ally would be hit" — don't cleanly map to how threat works now. Threat is declared and resolved as a phase, not an individual attack event. Players were confused about when to jump in.

**The fix:** Rewrite reaction triggers to reference threat phases explicitly.

**Draft language examples:**
- Old: "When an ally loses a hit die..."
- New: "When an ally faces threat higher than [X], you may react..."

- Old: "If the ally would be hit..."
- New: "Before threat resolves this round, you may..."

Core class abilities should especially reference this clearly, since they're always in the active area and should feel obviously useful in every encounter.

## SECONDARY CHANGES — HIGH VALUE, NEXT SPRINT AFTER THIS ONE

These are good ideas with clear merit. They are NOT urgent for the next playtest, but don't lose them.

---

### [ ] S1 — Warlock Pact Card: Partial Hit Die Spending

Players found the Pact (spend a hit die, add face value to roll) too punishing to use voluntarily. Losing a die entirely felt too binary.

**Consider:** "Reduce one hit die by 1–3 (your choice). Add that amount to your check." The die stays on the table but weakened. This keeps the gamble feeling without making it feel like pure self-harm.

**Alternative raised:** Spend resolve instead of hit dice. Worth testing.

---

### [ ] S2 — Make Every Non-Combat Check a Pooled Dice Challenge

Dan raised this and you agreed it has merit: what if you set a difficulty as GM (roll X dice, place them on the table) for any check — not just combat — and players collectively knock them down?

**Pros:** Consistent mechanic. Takes the door situation (which frustrated players) and gives it the same tactile feedback as combat. Players already love the dice-clearing interaction.

**Cons:** You lose the ability to keep DCs hidden. Worth testing on a dedicated non-combat scene.

**Your version:** You could still keep numeric DCs for things you want secret, and use the pool method when you want players to feel the tension visually. This is a GM tool, not a mandate.

---

### [ ] S3 — Clarify "Success vs Partial Success vs Failure" for Dice-Clearing

When you're clearing pooled dice (combat or skill check), the distinction between "I cleared it" vs "I reduced it" vs "I failed" needs to be codified somewhere players can reference. Right now it's implicit. Write one sentence, put it in the primer.

---

### [ ] S4 — Instinct Card Errata: Rewrite Niche Ones

Some Instinct cards (Primal especially) confused players at character creation because the flavor doesn't telegraph what you'd do. Rewrite any Instinct card where the player's first instinct (ha) is "when would I use this?"

These are real design gold. They are also confirmed distractions right now. Log them here, banish them from your brain until Playtest 3+.

### [ ] T1 — Universal Contested Check System
Every check becomes a dice pool the GM sets and players clear. Elegant. Needs a dedicated test session.

### [ ] T2 — NPC / Guest Cards in Player Decks
Princess Peanut Butter Cup going into the party's decks as a "bond with this NPC" card mid-quest. Escort mechanic via card draw. Genuinely exciting. Build a proper session around it.

### [ ] T3 — Clank-Style Stealth Encounter
Noise cards shuffled into decks at scene start. Drawing one means you make a sound. Security meter climbs toward failure. Bane-based. Design when you have a stealth session ready.

### [ ] T4 — Scar Cards
Already half-designed. Flesh out the card type with a few examples. Test in a multi-session campaign, not a one-shot.

### [ ] T5 — Leveling Structure / Tier Transitions
Drawing mechanics for leveling up, swapping cards between sessions, vault-style collection. A huge design space. Don't touch until you're past the core loop.

### [ ] T6 — Equipment / Armor as Class Core Cards
Fighter and Paladin getting resolve bonuses as class identity (not a character sheet). Good direction. Design alongside those classes specifically.

### [ ] T7 — Two-Flaw Flavor Split (Flaw vs Vice/Ideal)
The idea of one flaw being a weakness and one being a virtue-taken-too-far. Worth exploring when flaw design matures.

## OPEN DESIGN QUESTIONS (NOT ANSWERED YET)

These came up without resolution. Park them consciously.

- **How do Bond cards become Connection cards or coexist with them?** You said "both" but haven't fully landed on how two card types with similar mechanical effects justify their distinction. Clarity needed before print.
- **What is failure, exactly, in a dice-clearing system?** Dice that reduce a target by 1 vs dice that wiff entirely — does the Barbarian rage interact differently with each? Needs a single clean ruling.
- **How many cards in a starting deck?** You went from 20 → ~24 with these changes. Is 24 right? Test it. The number affects deck cycling speed and how often any given card comes up.
- **Scene transition timing:** GM should end a scene when players average 2 cards remaining. You said this in session; it needs to go in the GM guide or primer explicitly.
